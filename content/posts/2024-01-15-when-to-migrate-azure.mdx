Cloud migration is sold as a universal upgrade. It rarely is. After leading three enterprise migrations, two that delivered clear ROI and one that should have been postponed, I've developed a framework for making this decision honestly.

## The question nobody asks

Most migration conversations start with "how do we migrate?" The right first question is "should we migrate, and if so, what specifically?"

Azure is genuinely the right answer when:

- Your workloads have variable demand patterns that map well to elastic compute
- You need managed compliance tooling for regulated data (HIPAA, PCI, SOX)
- Your team's operational overhead on on-premises infrastructure is measurable and high
- You have active Microsoft enterprise agreements that make Azure cost-competitive

<Callout type="tip">
  The honest version: if you can't describe a specific operational problem that Azure solves, you're
  solving for technology selection, not business outcomes.
</Callout>

## When not to migrate

I've seen organizations migrate workloads that were stable, performant, and well-understood, only to spend eighteen months in hyperscaler support queues and FinOps meetings.

Avoid migration when:

- **The workload is static.** A reporting database that runs scheduled jobs doesn't benefit from autoscaling.
- **The team skill gap is large.** Cloud architecture requires different expertise than on-premises. Migrating without training is transferring risk, not reducing it.
- **The "old system" works.** Legacy doesn't mean broken. If a system is meeting SLAs and the team understands it, that's a business asset.

## The framework I actually use

Before recommending migration to a client, I walk through four questions:

1. **What is the specific pain point?** Can we measure it today?
2. **Is Azure the minimal viable fix?** Could a smaller change solve it?
3. **What does the 3-year TCO look like?** Cloud is not always cheaper.
4. **What does a rollback look like?** If the answer is "impossible," that's a risk to price in.

<Callout type="info">
  For a recent legacy system migration at a large international financial institution, the pain point was clear: 40% monthly downtime on a
  mission-critical financial platform. Azure was the right call. The migration delivered 95%
  downtime reduction in under 12 months.
</Callout>

## The framing that changes the conversation

"Cloud migration" frames the destination as the goal. I reframe it as "operational improvement with Azure as a possible path." That framing forces specificity, and specificity is what protects organizations from expensive migrations that don't move the needle.
